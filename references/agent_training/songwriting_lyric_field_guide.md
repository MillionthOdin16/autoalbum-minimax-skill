# Songwriting and Lyric Field Guide for Non-Expert Agents

Use this guide when creating or evaluating lyrics. The goal is not merely “good writing.” The goal is lyrics that a music model can render into compelling songs.

## What makes a lyric professional

A professional lyric usually has:

1. **A clear title idea**: the song has a phrase, image, command, question, or concept that can anchor memory.
2. **Section purpose**: intro sets tone, verse gives specifics, pre-chorus builds, chorus releases, bridge shifts, outro leaves residue.
3. **Singability/rapability**: lines fit natural breath and rhythm.
4. **Concrete details**: images, actions, objects, scenes, and voice-specific observations.
5. **Controlled repetition**: hook repeats enough to remember but not enough to become lazy.
6. **Progression**: later sections add pressure, contradiction, revelation, or consequence.
7. **Style alignment**: a drill verse, SZA-like alt-R&B verse, country chorus, and pop-punk bridge do not use the same diction or cadence.

## Section purposes

### [Intro]
Purpose: establish sonic/emotional world quickly. Can be wordless, a short hook phrase, a spoken fragment, a motif, or setup image.

Bad intro: long explanation of the album theme.
Good intro: one striking image, chant, or line that opens the door.

### [Verse]
Purpose: specific movement. It should contain new information, not repeat the chorus in prose.

Good verse ingredients:
- scene/action
- internal contradiction
- detail only this narrator would notice
- cadence matching the style

### [Pre Chorus]
Purpose: tension ramp. It should feel like the floor tilts toward the chorus.

Use:
- shorter lines
- rising urgency
- repeated syntax
- unresolved phrase
- question or threshold statement

### [Chorus] / [Hook]
Purpose: memory and emotional thesis.

Strong choruses are often:
- simpler than verses
- more repeatable
- more open-vowel
- emotionally direct
- rhythmically clear
- title-centered or title-adjacent

### [Post Chorus]
Purpose: earworm after the chorus. Often a chant, phrase, melodic syllable, or short repeated image.

### [Bridge]
Purpose: contrast. It should not merely be another verse. It changes harmony, perspective, tempo feel, vocal register, density, or meaning.

### [Break] / [Build Up] / [Inst] / [Solo]
Purpose: arrangement control. Use them to create space, reset energy, build tension, or feature a motif.

### [Outro]
Purpose: aftertaste. It can resolve, haunt, vanish, loop, or destabilize.

## Lyric quality heuristics

### Specific beats generic
Generic: “I’m broken but I keep going.”
Specific: “I taped the mirror where it cracked and shaved around the fault line.”

Generic: “You left me in the dark.”
Specific: “Your charger’s still tangled in the outlet by the stairs.”

### Chorus must be easier than verse
If the chorus is harder to remember than the verse, the song likely fails.

### Verse 2 must change the situation
Common fixes:
- reveal consequence
- switch from memory to present action
- answer Verse 1 with a worse truth
- move from private thought to public behavior
- introduce a new image that reframes the title

### Avoid false depth
False depth uses abstract nouns without sensory or dramatic pressure:
- destiny, pain, scars, demons, broken, numb, shadows, empty, forever

These can work only when grounded:
- “the shadow under your door kept moving” is stronger than “I’m lost in shadows.”

## Style-specific lyric posture

### Mainstream rap/trap
- punchy images
- confidence, threat, motion, flex, betrayal, pressure
- internal rhyme and cadence matter more than long metaphors
- hooks can be chants or repeated commands

### Conscious/cinematic rap
- narrative detail
- perspective shifts
- social/personal conflict
- more complex rhyme and syntax
- still needs a hook or memorable refrain

### Emo rap / melodic trap
- direct vulnerability
- melodic line lengths
- hook phrase with simple emotional charge
- guitar/piano/synth loop-friendly lyric rhythm

### Alt-R&B
- intimate details
- conversational asymmetry
- sensual/psychological ambiguity
- negative space and restraint
- chorus may be understated but must still hook

### Pop
- clear title
- clean emotional premise
- fast listener access
- simple hook with a twist
- bridge or final chorus lifts the meaning

### Country / folk
- story clarity
- objects and places
- conversational phrasing
- emotional turn near bridge/final chorus
- title often lands as punchline, moral, or image

### Rock / pop-punk
- directness, urgency, physical images
- big chorus with shouted/sung title
- bridge can be breakdown, confession, or gang vocal

## Red flags

- Every line has the same length.
- Verse sounds like a journal entry with no song shape.
- Chorus is too wordy.
- The title phrase never lands memorably.
- There are no nouns or actions, only emotions.
- “You” and “I” relationships are unclear.
- The song states the album thesis instead of dramatizing it.
- Lines require breath beyond the intended style.
- The lyrics contain production directions instead of lyrics.

## Revision moves

If the lyric is generic: add setting, object, action, contradiction.
If the hook is weak: simplify, repeat, place title phrase earlier, open vowels.
If verse 2 repeats: introduce consequence or reversal.
If prosody fails: shorten lines, change stress, insert rests, simplify consonant clusters.
If style fails: adjust diction and cadence to target lane.
