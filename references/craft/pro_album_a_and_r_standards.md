# Professional Album A&R Standards

## What professional-quality means here
The output should feel like an authored project, not a playlist of competent songs. The album must have a thesis, a sonic center, a style strategy, a reason for each track, and a sequence that rewards attention.

## First-three-tracks standard
Track 1 sets identity. Track 2 proves range. Track 3 should either confirm the lead-single promise or deepen the world. If the first three tracks do not make a listener trust the album, revise before rendering the rest.

## Track role standards
Every track must have one primary role:
- Opener: announces world, voice, and stakes.
- Promise builder: proves the album has range without breaking identity.
- Lead single: highest replay/hook balance.
- Deep cut: artistic depth or emotional specificity.
- Interlude/transition: changes oxygen, not filler.
- Rupture: breaks pattern with purpose.
- Climax: strongest emotional or musical arrival.
- Aftermath: consequence and reflection.
- Closer: final aftertaste; the line or feeling the listener carries away.

## Songcraft standards
- Verse must add information, pressure, image, or conflict.
- Hook must be memorable after one listen.
- Chorus must be simpler and more repeatable than the densest verse.
- Bridge/break must change perspective, harmony, energy, or texture.
- Arrangement must create contrast between sections.
- Lyric must be singable/rappable, not merely literary.

## Music theory and production standards
Use music theory as a control vocabulary, not decoration:
- key center and modal color guide mood,
- borrowed chords or non-diatonic colors should be purposeful,
- rhythmic density should rise or fall by section,
- chorus should often release melodic, rhythmic, or harmonic tension,
- bass behavior must match genre lane,
- drums must carry style identity,
- vocal delivery must be specified enough to steer MiniMax.

## Sequencing standards
A professional sequence manages:
- energy contrast,
- tempo contrast,
- lyrical perspective progression,
- sonic palette evolution,
- vocal fatigue,
- transitions and silence,
- placement of singles and experiments,
- final emotional landing.

## Final A&R questions
1. Does every track justify its position?
2. What is the weakest track and why is it still here?
3. Are any two adjacent tracks doing the same job?
4. Does the album have at least three undeniable moments?
5. Does the closer resolve, haunt, or deliberately rupture the thesis?
6. Could the style be identified without reading the prompt?
7. Is the best song placed where it helps the album most?
