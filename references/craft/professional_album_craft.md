# Professional Album Craft Reference

## Album-level principles

1. An album is not a playlist. Every track must either advance the thesis, deepen the sonic world, provide contrast, or earn the ending.
2. The first three tracks make the promise. The listener should understand the world, the voice, and the stakes before track four.
3. The closer is what the listener carries out of the room. It should resolve, rupture, or open a door intentionally.
4. Cohesion requires shared DNA; distinction requires each track to have its own job.
5. Sequencing is composition. Track order, silence, transition, key, tempo, and energy are creative decisions.

## Track-level principles

Every track needs:
- a role in the album.
- a non-interchangeable hook.
- a section-by-section energy map.
- a lyric thesis.
- a sonic reason to exist.
- a contrast mechanism relative to adjacent tracks.
- at least one memorable moment: lyric, hook, riff, break, drop, vocal gesture, or production turn.

## Songwriting and lyric craft

- Verse = detail, tension, scene, setup.
- Pre-chorus = pressure, lift, narrowing into the chorus.
- Chorus/hook = emotional thesis, simplest language, strongest repeatability.
- Post-chorus = earworm or aftershock.
- Bridge/break = contrast and recontextualization.
- Outro = exit image, question, ritual, or release.

Use concrete image before abstract meaning. Avoid generic emotional labels when a physical detail can imply the emotion.

## Singability checklist

- Chorus lines are shorter, clearer, and more vowel-open than verse lines.
- Dense consonant clusters are avoided in hook anchors.
- Important title phrase lands on a singable vowel if possible.
- Verse rhythm can be denser; chorus should breathe.
- Lines are not all the same length.
- The hook can be remembered after one listen.
- The title or title-adjacent phrase appears naturally in the hook unless intentionally withheld.

## Music-theory control layer

AutoAlbum should not over-specify theory that MiniMax may ignore, but it should use theory to make better prompt packets.

Useful controls:
- key center: major/minor/modal emotional color.
- relative major/minor shifts across album for light/dark motion.
- borrowed chord or modal interchange prompt language for one signature turn.
- harmonic rhythm: slow, hypnotic loops vs active progressions.
- chorus lift: brighter harmony, wider vocal register, or fuller instrumentation.
- bridge contrast: new key center, sparser texture, alternate groove, or harmonic surprise.
- bass/key awareness: choose keys and registers that serve low end and vocal identity.

## Arrangement and production controls

Use energy maps, not just section names.

For each section specify:
- energy level 1-9.
- vocal density.
- drum/groove state.
- bass/sub state.
- harmonic texture.
- transition mechanism.

Contrast tools:
- remove drums before chorus.
- withhold bass until hook.
- change phrase length.
- switch from short/choppy verse lines to long/open chorus lines.
- use silence, break, or instrumental transition.
- introduce one new texture after verse two.
- use a bridge to shift harmony, rhythm, or point of view.

## Anti-generic album tests

For every track ask:
1. What makes this track unmistakably part of this album?
2. What makes this track impossible to confuse with any other track here?
3. What would be lost if this track were cut?
4. What is the one moment a listener would remember?
5. Does the track create a new shade, or merely repeat a prior track?

## Professional iteration process

- Generate many concepts, then select.
- Write before judging, then judge ruthlessly.
- Create multiple candidate generations per key track.
- Compare candidates by listening impact, not prompt beauty.
- Re-sequence after audio exists.
- Cut weak tracks, even if they fit the concept on paper.
